I never understood why maths was neccessary in real life, especially the ones about cosines and sines. I learnt enough to pass through school and college, but never loved the subject.

Maths is the Physical World on Paper

I have always been intrigued by one property of mathematics and physics, how equaations and accurately predict physical outcomes and how certian quantities relate so beautifully to each other.

Consider the famous equation \( E=mc^2 \). Everytime I used to derive this in school, I used to be in awe on how symbols could have meaning that could mesh together to get into this exact formula. We would start out with the formula for one dimensional motion, and after some derivations, integrations we would arrive at the formulat for making a Nuclear Bomb. Even though this was fascinating enough, I never really gave it any thought in school.

Everytime I would come across equations and numbers, mostly on Fermats Library that remotely mirror this formula. I would always think how one formula flows effortesly onto another, how and why is so beautiful. Equations of motion can be dervied from one another, while making sense therotically.

While during my zoning out in the middle of the day times I was thinking about numbers and really wanted to know why exactly \(2+2 = 4\), and what is the deeper meaning of mathematics. I came to the conclusion that \(2+2=4\) is so because \(4=2+2\). Let me elaborate.

Consider an apple. Now consider another apple. How many apples do we have now? We have \(2\) apples. So what is \(2\) then? \(2\) symbolically represents the amount of apples we have when we take one apple and another apple. Let's say we hae another \(2\) apples, so the total number of apples we have is \(4\). So in that respect if we take one apple, then another, then another, and finally another, \(4\) denotes the thing that we have now.

Maths is then an abstraction on top of the physical world. It's a way for us to work with symbols that can be easily manipulated
